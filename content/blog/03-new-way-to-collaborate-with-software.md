# A New Way to Collaborate With Software

Software used to be a thing you built.

Then it became a thing you built *with* — pair programming, code review, design docs, the slow accumulation of a shared mental model around a shared repo.

Now it's becoming something else. Something we don't have a word for yet.

Most of us feel it before we can name it. Agents are writing more of the code. Pull requests are landing faster than humans can read them. Three teammates can ship three correct, working features in an afternoon and still not agree on what the product actually does. The bottleneck quietly shifted, and almost nobody noticed where it shifted *to*.

It moved upstream of the code.

That's what we kept circling in our huddle this week. Not the writing of software. The *colluding* about software. The part where two engineers — or three, or thirty — try to mean the same thing at the same time, before the agents go off and turn that meaning into a thousand lines of perfectly typed code that may or may not be what anyone actually wanted.

In our huddle that turned into one sentence we kept saying out loud: we're solving organizational alignment, not just traditional code review.

And once you see that as the problem, the rest of the stack rearranges itself.

There's another phrase that came up, and it stuck: a new way to collaborate with software.

Read it twice. The preposition is doing all the work. Not *on* software. *With* it. The software is no longer the artifact you're collaborating about. It's now one of the parties at the table.

That sounds abstract until you watch it happen. An engineer opens a draft. An agent has already written a first pass — a walkthrough, a sequence diagram, a view of which services moved. The engineer reads it, edits the framing in their voice, marks what they personally verified, and adds the one piece of context the agent couldn't infer. What lands on the next person's screen is human-verified content, not a machine guess wearing a confident voice.

Then they paste a link in Slack, and a teammate three time zones away reacts to it before lunch.

Notice what didn't happen.

Nobody scheduled a meeting. Nobody opened a 600-file diff. Nobody DM'd "got a sec?" Nobody apologized for the length of the ask. Nobody burned a favor they were saving for something more important. In our huddle this week we put a name on that quiet tax — burning social capital — and once you have a name for it, you start counting how often it shows up. The answer is: constantly.

Something cheaper than review just happened. Something that doesn't have a name yet, but probably will soon.

We've been calling it a code trail, but the format is almost beside the point. What matters is the physics. The high-cost part of asking — the part where the asker has to bundle up enough context for the answerer to weigh in — used to happen *during* the conversation, eating both people's attention. Now it happens *before*, mostly by the agent, mostly while you're doing something else. The trail does the work of explaining code clearly and quickly, allowing for easy sharing and rapid understanding. By the time a human opens it, the context is already paged in for them. They're not being asked to dig. They're being asked to nod, push back, or steer.

That's the shift. Not faster review. *Cheaper asking*.

And the second-order effects are wild.

Senior engineers, who used to be the bottleneck on every interesting decision, suddenly have headroom they haven't had in years — because the cost of pinging them dropped by an order of magnitude, and the quality of what shows up in their inbox went up. Junior engineers, who used to hoard their questions because each one chipped at their reputation, are asking three, four, five times more often — and learning correspondingly faster. Cross-team conversations that used to require a 30-minute meeting now happen as one paste, one scroll, one reply.

That's what it looks like to help teams quickly understand project intent and maintain momentum in collaborative environments — not as a tagline, as a Tuesday.

A team's tempo isn't set by how fast it can write code. It's set by how fast it can agree.

For most of software's history, those two were tangled — agreement happened *through* the code, in PR comments and review cycles and the slow embodied wisdom of senior reviewers who'd seen this exact pattern before. That was fine when humans wrote everything. It is dangerously inadequate when humans write almost none of it.

One of us said it bluntly in the huddle: once you align on intent, the implementation is straightforward for the agent. The hard part stopped being the code. The hard part is now the alignment. Now that coding has been solved, code trails will solve alignment to make dependable software factories possible.

That's not a marketing line. That's a diagnosis.

The teams who survive the agent era will not be the teams with the best models. The teams who survive will be the teams who figured out how to compress the distance between "I wrote this" and "we ship this" without losing the part where a person actually had to nod.

That compression is the product. The trail is the surface. The agent is the engine. But the thing in the middle, the thing that makes the whole stack go, is the new contract we're slowly writing between humans and the software they used to fully author and now only partly do.

A new way to collaborate with software.

Not with each other, mediated by software. Not on software, as a craft.

*With* software. As a peer.

We're going to look back at this moment the way the early internet generation looks back at email — a thing so obvious now that nobody remembers it was a strange idea at first. *You're going to talk to who? Through a wire? Without a meeting?*

The strange idea this time is even simpler.

You're going to ask software what it thinks. It's going to tell you. A human is going to weigh in. Another human is going to weigh in. And somewhere in that loop, a decision is going to get made faster, with more people aligned to it, than any team has shipped before.

That's the bet.

That's the product.

That's the new way.
